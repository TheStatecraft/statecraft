My Experience Using an Intel Mac in 2026

"After just one week of using it, I'm genuinely shocked - not because it's perfect, but because it has completely changed my expectations of what an Intel Mac can still offer in 2026."

As a long-time Apple user, I’ve always been interested in iMacs. From their all-in-one design and beautiful displays, to the elegance they bring to a desk, they’ve always been iconic computers. But I’d always chosen to use MacBooks connected to external monitors - until now.

After endless research on Apple Silicon iMac Pro rumours, the current 24-inch iMac, and used 27-inch iMacs, I bought a 2020 27-inch 5K iMac. I bought it knowing it wouldn’t replace my M5 MacBook Pro. The two machines serve very different purposes, and the M5 significantly outperforms the Intel iMac in demanding workloads.

After just one week of use, I am genuinely shocked.

Immediately after pressing the power button on the rear, the bass-filled speakers blew my mind with the classic startup chime. They are better than any other speakers I’ve used - even my AirPods Max. Previously, I’d used a pair of Creative Pebble v2s. And while they are adequate, they are nowhere near as clear and deep as the iMac’s speakers. The speakers immensely contribute to the premium sensation of the machine. It’s obvious Apple designed the iMac to feel like a complete desktop experience, where everything you need is in the box, rather than a computer attached to a screen.

Not only did the speakers shock me, but the display instantly blessed my retinas. The crisp, bright, and colour-accurate display was like nothing I’d ever used before. Yes, I was already familiar with the screen on my MacBook Pro, but the iMac’s is unmatched. The sheer sharpness of the 5K resolution stood out like no other. Until now, I’d been using a 1440p monitor, so I quadrupled the amount of pixels on my desk. It felt as if I was staring into a window. The computer may be using six-year-old technology, but the display certainly isn’t.

Despite its age, I rarely found myself waiting for the infamous beach ball. Applications opened quickly, multitasking remained smooth, and the iMac never felt like the obsolete machine many people claim it to be. Compared to my silent MacBook, I noticed the fans are prone to spinning occasionally but it’s never loud - even when gaming.

One feature I hadn’t appreciated until returning to Intel was Boot Camp. Being able to run Windows natively alongside macOS is something Apple Silicon Macs simply can’t do. While I still have a dedicated gaming PC, it was refreshing to install part of my Steam library and know the option was there. The games ran smoothly, although not as well as my PC, which is brilliant for an iMac of this age.

I wouldn’t class myself as a ‘heavy user’ when it comes to workloads, but I can use various demanding apps. This influenced my decision to use 16GB memory, for now, as it should be enough for my needs, and it is one of the last upgradable Macs. I didn’t opt for a more powerful GPU such as the RX 5700XT as I simply didn’t need it. Instead, I got the RX 5300.

With the recent announcement of macOS Golden Gate, this iMac will no longer be officially supported by Apple, meaning macOS Tahoe will be the final version this iMac receives. However, it will still receive two-years of security updates. I bought this iMac knowing support wouldn’t last for much longer. But I accepted that compromise as Intel Macs aren’t obsolete - Apple have been known for their outstanding device longevity. Most people, myself included, simply don’t currently need the performance of the M5 chip. And when plenty of software still runs well, it was a bargain for under £400. I believe this iMac, whilst older, is still perfect for many users. If people want an iMac without the £1499 price tag, want an iMac with native Windows compatibility, or simply prefer Intel Macs, I would still recommend it in 2026 providing they are light users and aren’t expecting another 5 years of software updates.

Would I buy it again in 2026? Absolutely. Apple Silicon is undoubtedly the future, and for many people it’s the better choice. But after a week with the Intel iMac, I’m convinced its death has been greatly exaggerated.
